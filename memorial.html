A Life Remembered

Katrina was more than a name. She was a presence—someone who could light up a room with her laughter, challenge you with her mind, and remind you what it meant to be fully alive. Her spirit was electric, her heart was vast, and her loss left a wound that will never fully heal.

She was taken too soon by fentanyl—a substance that has claimed too many lives, destroyed too many families, and left too many questions unanswered. Her death was not a statistic. It was a tragedy. And it is the reason 1142 LABS exists.

Why We Remember

Katrina's death was preventable. The opioid crisis is not an accident—it is a failure of policy, compassion, and understanding. 1142 LABS was founded in the wake of her loss, driven by the belief that knowledge, harm reduction, and honest conversation can save lives. We remember Katrina not just with grief, but with action. Every research breakthrough, every tool built, every life helped—it's all for her.

She would have loved this work. She would have challenged it, improved it, and made it better. In her absence, we carry that torch. We ask the hard questions. We refuse to look away. We honor her by refusing to let anyone else become a statistic.

The Opioid Crisis: A National Emergency

Fentanyl is a synthetic opioid 50-100 times more potent than morphine. It is cheap, easy to produce, and deadly. A dose the size of a few grains of salt can kill. It is often mixed into other drugs without the user's knowledge, turning every pill, every powder, into a game of Russian roulette.

The opioid epidemic has claimed over 500,000 lives in the United States since 1999. In Canada, opioid-related deaths have increased by over 400% in the past decade. Fentanyl is now the leading cause of overdose deaths in North America.

This is not a moral failing. This is a public health crisis. People who use drugs are not criminals—they are human beings who deserve compassion, support, and access to life-saving resources.

Harm Reduction Resources

  • Naloxone (Narcan): A life-saving medication that reverses opioid overdoses. Available at most pharmacies without a prescription.
  • Fentanyl Test Strips: Inexpensive strips that detect fentanyl in drugs before use. Available through harm reduction organizations.
  • Never Use Alone: Call 1-800-484-3731 (US) or use the Never Use Alone hotline for supervised use.
  • SAMHSA National Helpline: 1-800-662-4357 (24/7 confidential support for substance use disorders)
  • Local Harm Reduction Centers: Many cities have needle exchanges, supervised consumption sites, and free naloxone distribution.

If you or someone you know is struggling with substance use, you are not alone. Help is available. Recovery is possible. And every life is worth saving.
